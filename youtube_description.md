## DESCRIPTION (copy-paste ready)

```
Every year of global temperature since 1781 — ranked and racing.

Watch the coldest years of the 1800s slowly give way to the record-breaking heat of the 2020s. Each bar shows the absolute global mean temperature for that year, colored from deep blue (cool) to deep red (hot), and compared to the 1951–1980 climate baseline.

📊 What you're seeing:
• Bar chart race: the top 10 hottest years recorded so far, updating as each new year is added
• Time-series line: every year plotted continuously from 1781 to 2025
• Markers: major volcanic eruptions, population milestones, and turning points that shaped the climate record

🌡️ Units: Fahrenheit primary, Celsius in parentheses. Anomalies compared to the 1951–1980 global mean (57.2°F / 14.0°C).

─────────────────────────────

📌 FACTOID REFERENCES

Each marker in the video corresponds to a real event. Sources below:

🌋 1783 — Laki Eruption
Iceland's Laki fissure erupted for 8 months, injecting sulfur dioxide into the stratosphere and cooling the Northern Hemisphere for two years.
→ Grattan & Brayshay (1995), The Geographical Journal: https://doi.org/10.2307/3060016

👥 1800 — World Population: 1 Billion
→ UN Population Division, World Population Prospects: https://population.un.org/wpp/

🌋 1815 — Mt. Tambora / Year Without a Summer
The largest volcanic eruption in recorded history. 1816 became the "Year Without a Summer" — crop failures across Europe and North America.
→ Stommel & Stommel (1979), Scientific American: https://doi.org/10.1038/scientificamerican0679-176

🚂 1825 — First Steam Railway
The Stockton & Darlington Railway opened in England, marking the start of the coal-powered transportation age.
→ National Railway Museum, York: https://www.railwaymuseum.org.uk/

🛢️ 1859 — First Oil Well (US)
Edwin Drake's well in Titusville, Pennsylvania, launched the commercial petroleum industry.
→ Drake Well Museum: https://drakewell.org/

🌋 1883 — Krakatoa Eruption
The eruption generated the loudest sound in recorded history and dropped global temperatures for two years.
→ Self & Rampino (1981), Nature: https://doi.org/10.1038/294699a0

🚗 1890 — The Car Age Begins
Karl Benz's Patent-Motorwagen entered commercial production in the late 1880s; gasoline automobiles were being sold commercially by 1890.
→ Daimler AG Corporate History: https://www.daimler.com/company/tradition/company-history/

🌋 1912 — Mt. Katmai Eruption
One of the largest volcanic eruptions of the 20th century, in Alaska.
→ Hildreth & Fierstein (2000), GSA Bulletin: https://doi.org/10.1130/0016-7606(2000)112<1594:KVCATG>2.0.CO;2

👥 1927 — World Population: 2 Billion
→ UN Population Division: https://population.un.org/wpp/

⚔️ 1939 — WWII Industrial Surge
War production dramatically accelerated coal and industrial output across all major economies.
→ Stern (2005), global sulfur emissions, Chemosphere: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.chemosphere.2004.08.022

☢️ 1945 — The Nuclear Age Begins
The Trinity test (July 16, 1945) was the first detonation of a nuclear device.
→ National Security Archive, Manhattan Project: https://nsarchive.gwu.edu/nukevault/ebb140/

🌋 1964 — Mt. Agung Eruption
Agung's 1963–64 eruption cooled global temperatures by approximately 0.5°C for two years.
→ Hansen et al. (1992), GRL: https://doi.org/10.1029/91GL02788

🌾 1968 — Green Revolution
Norman Borlaug's high-yield wheat varieties, combined with synthetic fertilizers and irrigation, tripled global food production between the 1950s and 1990s.
→ Borlaug, N.E. (1970), Nobel Prize Lecture: https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/peace/1970/borlaug/lecture/

🌊 1976 — Pacific Climate Shift
A sudden reorganization of Pacific Ocean circulation patterns (the Pacific Decadal Oscillation flipped phase), influencing global temperature trends for decades afterward.
→ Trenberth & Hurrell (1994), Climate Dynamics: https://doi.org/10.1007/BF00204745

👥 1987 — World Population: 5 Billion
→ UN Population Division: https://population.un.org/wpp/

🌋 1991 — Mt. Pinatubo Eruption
The second-largest volcanic eruption of the 20th century, cooling global temperatures by ~0.6°C for two years.
→ Hansen et al. (1992), GRL: https://doi.org/10.1029/91GL02788

🔥 1998 — Monster El Niño
The strongest El Niño on record to that point pushed 1998 to the top of the temperature rankings — where it stayed for seven years.
→ Trenberth (1997), BAMS: https://doi.org/10.1175/1520-0477(1997)078<2771:TDOENO>2.0.CO;2
→ NASA GISTEMP: https://data.giss.nasa.gov/gistemp/

📈 2005 — 1998's Record Broken
2005 dethroned 1998 as the hottest year on record.
→ Hansen et al. (2006), PNAS: https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.0606291103

🌡️ 2010 — Russia Heat Wave / Pakistan Floods
In the same month (July–August 2010), Russia experienced its deadliest heat wave and Pakistan its worst floods in recorded history.
→ Barriopedro et al. (2011), Science: https://doi.org/10.1126/science.1201224

🔥 2016 — All Records Shattered
Boosted by a strong El Niño, 2016 ran +1.2°C above the pre-industrial average — a new global record by a wide margin.
→ NASA/NOAA press release (2017): https://www.nasa.gov/press-release/nasa-noaa-data-show-2016-warmest-year-on-record-globally

🔥 2019 — Amazon & Australian Mega-Fires
An estimated 18 million hectares burned across the Amazon and Australia in a single fire season.
→ Copernicus Atmosphere Monitoring Service (CAMS): https://atmosphere.copernicus.eu/

🚨 2024 — First Year Above 1.5°C Paris Threshold
2024 became the first full calendar year to average more than 1.5°C above the pre-industrial baseline — the target set by the 2015 Paris Agreement.
→ Copernicus Climate Change Service (C3S), Global Climate Highlights 2024: https://climate.copernicus.eu/global-climate-highlights-2024
```
